\section{Results}

The results report WAPE as a diagnostic forecast metric. The default normalized planning objective excludes direct forecast-error terms and combines inventory exposure, planning volatility, execution burden, and model-switching burden. Oracle gaps are reported in two forms: gap to the Realized-Inventory Oracle DP, which isolates non-deployable model-selection information over the same forecast candidates, and gap to the Realized Demand Oracle, which also reflects forecast uncertainty. The codebase also includes a Full-Outcome Oracle DP benchmark for explicit forecast-error sensitivity settings; final result tables will be regenerated before any claims are made from that benchmark.

\subsection{Result 1: Execution Feasibility Is Empirically Measurable}

The DataCo analysis shows that execution risk is observable in public supply-chain execution data. The generated calibration table reports a global late-delivery rate of 0.548, with context-level late-delivery-rate percentiles of 0.535 at the 30th percentile, 0.552 at the median, 0.581 at the 75th percentile, and 0.901 at the 95th percentile. These values are not interpreted as direct economic costs for the retail demand datasets. They are used to construct data-informed execution-risk sensitivity scenarios.

DataCo is therefore an execution-risk scenario anchor rather than an exact cost-calibration source. The paper transfers the observed heterogeneity of late-delivery risk into relative execution-risk regimes; it does not claim that a DataCo late-delivery percentage is the dollar cost of an inventory execution violation in Favorita, M5, or Walmart.

\subsection{Result 2: Accuracy-First Methods Remain Strong Forecasters but Can Create Execution Burden}

The Favorita proof of concept confirms that accuracy-first forecasting remains important. In the generated forecast metrics, LightGBM has the lowest test WAPE among the evaluated Favorita forecasting models, with WAPE 0.109. XGBoost is close at 0.111, while transparent baselines such as seasonal naive, moving average, exponential smoothing, and naive forecasts have larger test WAPE values. This result supports the use of LightGBM or Global Best as a strong accuracy-first reference rather than as a weak strawman.

However, the method-family planning table shows that the accuracy-first Global Best strategy has substantial execution burden in the baseline Favorita scenario. Global Best has WAPE 0.109, inventory cost 3,010,925, planning volatility 414.964, execution penalty 546,133, execution violation rate 0.284, and normalized total loss 1.550. These values make clear that a strong forecaster can still create material planning adjustment burden.

\subsection{Result 3: DataCo-Informed Execution Scenarios Reorder Deployable Strategy Rankings}

Table~\ref{tab:favorita-method-family-summary} reports representative Favorita strategy families across DataCo-informed execution scenarios. Under the baseline scenario, Operational Ensemble has WAPE 0.119, execution penalty 118,441, violation rate 0.133, and normalized total loss 1.422. Simple Ensemble has worse WAPE at 0.129 but lower execution penalty at 47,421 and lower violation rate at 0.066, with normalized total loss 1.434. Both are less accurate than Global Best, yet both reduce execution burden substantially.

As execution-risk sensitivity increases, the ranking changes more clearly. In the severe DataCo-informed scenario, Simple Ensemble has normalized total loss 1.473 and Operational Ensemble has normalized total loss 1.496, while Global Best has normalized total loss 2.000. This does not imply that ensembles universally dominate. It indicates that, under execution-sensitive objectives, the deployable strategy ranking can differ from the forecast-accuracy ranking.

\subsection{Result 4: Smoothing and Ensemble Methods Occupy Different Parts of the Deployability Frontier}

The improved Favorita comparison suggests that smoothing and ensembles reduce different forms of operational burden. In the baseline Favorita scenario, Smoothed Alpha 0.25 has WAPE 0.109, nearly identical to Global Best, and reduces execution penalty to 102 with violation rate 0.001. The tradeoff is higher inventory cost, 4,515,961, compared with 3,010,925 for Global Best. Utility Alpha further suppresses execution penalty to zero in the baseline table, but with even higher inventory cost. These patterns are consistent with the interpretation that smoothing can make plans more executable by adapting gradually, but it does not eliminate inventory tradeoffs.

Ensembles occupy a different part of the frontier. Operational Ensemble has a more moderate accuracy penalty than Simple Ensemble and substantially lower execution penalty than Global Best. Simple Ensemble has the lowest execution penalty among the ensemble references but worse WAPE. The current evidence therefore supports a tradeoff interpretation rather than a universal winner interpretation.

\subsection{Result 5: M5 Confirms Robustness in Hierarchical Demand Planning}

M5 provides a larger hierarchical robustness setting. In quick-mode item-store replication, the Simple Ensemble has the lowest non-oracle WAPE at 0.332, but it also has execution penalty 4,451 and violation rate 0.233. Smoothed Alpha 0.25 has WAPE 0.368, execution penalty 101, violation rate 0.010, and normalized total loss 1.223 in the large-scale replication table. Operational Ensemble has WAPE 0.356, execution penalty 302, violation rate 0.024, and normalized total loss 1.531. Global Best has WAPE 0.368, execution penalty 1,279, violation rate 0.089, and normalized total loss 2.500.

These results reinforce the main thesis. The method with the lowest WAPE is not necessarily the best deployable strategy once execution burden is included. At the same time, the M5 results do not imply that smoothing always dominates; they show that controlled adaptation is favorable in this particular robustness setting and under the current normalized planning-loss design.

\subsection{Result 6: Planning Granularity Changes the Preferred Deployable Method}

The M5 hierarchy sensitivity results show that planning granularity changes the preferred deployable method. At the item-store grain, Smoothed Alpha 0.25 has the lowest normalized total loss among deployable methods. At the department-store grain, Operational Ensemble has normalized total loss 1.177, ranking ahead of Simple Ensemble and Smoothed Alpha 0.25. At the category-store grain, Operational Ensemble and Simple Ensemble both have normalized total loss around 1.261, while Global Best has normalized total loss 2.500. This pattern suggests that aggregation can change the relative value of smoothing versus ensemble composition.

\subsection{Result 7: Intermittent-Demand Stress Supports Controlled Adaptation}

The M5 intermittent-demand stress test further supports controlled adaptation. Across low-, medium-, and high-intermittency buckets, Smoothed Alpha 0.25 has the lowest normalized total loss in the generated table. In the high-intermittency bucket, Smoothed Alpha 0.25 has normalized total loss 1.284, while Global Best has 2.500 and Simple Ensemble has 4.470. In the low-intermittency bucket, Smoothed Alpha 0.25 and Operational Ensemble are close, with normalized total losses 1.156 and 1.162, respectively. These results should be interpreted as evidence that demand intermittency changes the feasibility tradeoff, not as proof that one smoothing parameter is universally optimal.

\subsection{Result 8: Walmart Business-Context Robustness}

Walmart is used to test whether weekly business-context signals alter deployable strategy rankings. The current outputs compare history-only candidate forecasts with history-plus-context candidate forecasts, then evaluate the same decision-layer strategies under DataCo-informed execution scenarios. The evidence is mixed in a useful way. In the baseline history-plus-context setting, Global Best remains a strong deployable reference, with WAPE 0.057 and normalized total loss 1.150. Budgeted DP and DP Feasibility have similar WAPE, lower execution violation rates, and lower execution penalties, but their switch-count and inventory tradeoffs keep them behind Global Best under the baseline weight setting.

The ranking changes when execution-risk sensitivity increases. Under the DataCo severe scenario with history-plus-context forecasts, Budgeted DP has normalized total loss 1.452, execution penalty 33,796, and execution violation rate 0.009, while Global Best has normalized total loss 1.600, execution penalty 56,342, and violation rate 0.028. This does not mean that business context always makes feasibility-aware selection dominate. It shows that, once execution risk is weighted more heavily, a finite-horizon or budgeted decision layer can become preferable even when the accuracy-first baseline remains highly competitive on WAPE.

The context-aware versus context-free comparison also suggests that context features should be interpreted as robustness inputs rather than automatic improvements. For Global Best, adding context slightly worsens WAPE in the baseline comparison but reduces execution penalty and violation rate. For DP-based strategies, context slightly improves WAPE and reduces execution burden, while normalized total loss changes only modestly. These patterns support the paper's central claim: the value of business context is clearest when it is evaluated at the forecast-to-plan layer, not only through forecast error.

\subsection{Result 9: Switch-Budget Sensitivity Quantifies the Cost of Strict Model Governance}

The switch-budget sensitivity sweep evaluates Budgeted DP under $K \in \{0,1,2,4,8\}$ for Walmart and M5 quick-mode settings. The generated results show that increasing the switch budget does not mechanically improve normalized operational loss. In Walmart, Budgeted DP has normalized total loss 1.206 at $K=0$, 1.199 at $K=1$, and 1.229 at $K=8$, while model-switch count rises from 0 to 241. In M5, Budgeted DP improves WAPE from 0.369 at $K=0$ to 0.353 at $K=8$, but model-switch count rises from 0 to 590 and the lowest Budgeted-DP normalized loss in the sweep occurs at $K=2$. This pattern supports the interpretation that $K$ controls a governance and execution-feasibility tradeoff rather than serving as a monotone performance knob.
