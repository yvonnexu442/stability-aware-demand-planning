\section{Introduction}

Industrial demand-planning systems increasingly rely on predictive models to support replenishment, inventory positioning, labor planning, transportation commitments, and capacity allocation. Recent reviews show that artificial intelligence and machine learning are becoming more visible in supply-chain planning and demand-planning practice (Walter et al. 2025; Culot et al. 2024; Vlachos and Reddy 2025). Forecasting benchmarks and time-series foundation models further show that predictive capability is advancing rapidly (Makridakis et al. 2022; Das et al. 2024; Ansari et al. 2024). These advances create value, but they also sharpen an operations-management problem: better forecast accuracy does not automatically mean better deployment quality.

Operations execute planning signals, not forecast accuracy metrics. A planner cannot execute WAPE or RMSE. The operation executes an inventory target, a replenishment order, a staffing plan, a production target, or a transportation commitment. When a forecast-driven plan changes sharply, downstream systems may need supplier coordination, warehouse rebalancing, labor rescheduling, or enterprise planning-system updates. These changes can be costly even when the forecast itself is numerically accurate.

This creates a planning-execution gap. Forecasting models may adapt daily or weekly, while procurement lead times, replenishment workflows, planner governance, and execution infrastructure may absorb only gradual change. A forecast-driven plan can therefore create planning volatility, model-switching burden, and execution-capacity violations. Standard forecasting evaluation often emphasizes WAPE, MAE, RMSE, or related accuracy metrics, while deployment requires feasibility-adjusted planning utility.

This paper studies feasibility-constrained model selection for operational demand planning. The central thesis is that forecasting-driven strategies should be selected by operational feasibility as well as prediction accuracy. Candidate forecasts are converted into planning signals, evaluated against inventory exposure and execution constraints, and compared through deployable decision-layer policies. The empirical contribution is to measure how often and by how much accuracy-first selection differs from operational-loss-optimal deployable selection.

The study uses four public-data roles. DataCo supply-chain execution records are used to anchor execution-risk scenarios from observed late-delivery heterogeneity. Favorita daily retail demand data provide the main forecast-to-plan proof of concept. M5 retail data provide hierarchy and intermittent-demand robustness. Walmart weekly sales data provide business-context robustness through history-only versus history-plus-context forecasts, holiday and markdown windows, weekly cadence constraints, and switch-budget sensitivity. These datasets are used to evaluate whether the planning-execution gap remains visible under different operational conditions; they are not used to claim that one forecasting model universally dominates.

The paper contributes to IEOM-oriented operations research in four ways. First, it defines a forecast-to-plan evaluation layer that reports forecast accuracy separately from operational planning utility. Second, it operationalizes the planning-execution gap through execution violation rate and planning-execution gap rate. Third, it compares accuracy-first, ensemble, smoothing, greedy, dynamic programming, and budgeted dynamic programming deployable strategies. Fourth, it validates the tradeoff using DataCo-informed scenarios, Favorita proof-of-concept results, M5 hierarchy/intermittency robustness, and Walmart business-context robustness.

\subsection{Objectives}

The specific objectives of this study are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Define a forecast-to-plan evaluation layer that measures operational feasibility in addition to WAPE.
    \item Operationalize the planning-execution gap using execution violation rate and planning-execution gap rate.
    \item Use DataCo execution records to construct data-informed execution-risk scenarios.
    \item Compare accuracy-first, ensemble, smoothing, greedy, DP, and budgeted-DP deployable planning strategies.
    \item Validate the planning-execution gap across Favorita, M5, and Walmart and translate the findings into practical planning recommendations.
\end{enumerate}
