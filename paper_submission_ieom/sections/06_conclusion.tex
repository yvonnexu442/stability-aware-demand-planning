\section{Conclusion}

This paper reframes operational demand planning as feasibility-constrained model selection. The planning-execution gap was quantified by comparing accuracy-first model selection with operational-loss-optimal deployable planning selection. Across 123 paper-facing evaluation groups, the accuracy-first deployable method differs from the operational-loss-best deployable method in 91.1\% of cases. The accuracy-first planning-execution gap rate is 15.4\%, while the best deployable operational-loss strategy has a gap rate of 2.7\%.

Objective 1 was addressed by defining a forecast-to-plan evaluation layer that reports WAPE separately from inventory exposure, volatility, switching burden, and execution violations. Objective 2 was addressed by defining planning-execution gap rate. Objective 3 was addressed by using DataCo execution records to construct data-informed execution-risk scenarios. Objective 4 was addressed by comparing accuracy-first, ensemble, smoothing, greedy, DP, and budgeted-DP deployable strategies. Objective 5 was addressed through Favorita, M5, and Walmart validation and through evidence-linked planning recommendations.

The results show that execution-risk scenarios can change deployable strategy ranking, that hierarchy and intermittency can change the preferred mechanism, and that weekly business context can affect feasibility tradeoffs. The practical implication is direct: planners should evaluate deployment feasibility before model rollout. Forecast accuracy remains important, but it should be interpreted as a diagnostic metric rather than as the sole deployment objective.
