\begin{center}
\textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}

Operational demand planning increasingly relies on predictive models, but operations execute planning signals rather than forecast accuracy metrics. A numerically accurate forecast can still produce volatile inventory targets, frequent switching, or execution violations when downstream systems cannot absorb abrupt plan changes. This paper formulates forecast-driven demand planning as feasibility-constrained model selection. Candidate forecasts are converted into executable planning signals and evaluated using WAPE, inventory exposure, planning volatility, switching burden, execution violations, normalized planning loss, greedy-to-DP gap, and oracle gaps. DataCo execution records provide data-informed execution-risk scenarios, Favorita retail demand data provide the main forecast-to-plan proof of concept, M5 retail data test hierarchy and intermittent-demand robustness, and Walmart weekly sales data test business-context robustness. Across 123 paper-facing evaluation groups, the accuracy-first deployable strategy differs from the operational-loss-optimal deployable strategy in 91.1\% of cases. The accuracy-first planning-execution gap rate is 15.4\%, compared with 2.7\% for the best deployable operational-loss strategy. Favorita results show ranking shifts under severe execution-risk scenarios, M5 results show that planning grain and intermittency change the preferred deployable mechanism, and Walmart results show that business context and cadence constraints affect deployable rankings. The contribution is not a new forecasting model. It is an interpretable operations decision layer showing that forecast accuracy should be treated as a diagnostic metric, while deployment should be evaluated by feasibility-adjusted planning utility.

\textbf{Keywords}
