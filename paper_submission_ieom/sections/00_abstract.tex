\begin{center}
\textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}

Firms increasingly use predictive demand models to set inventory targets, replenishment plans, and execution commitments. However, operations execute planning signals rather than forecast accuracy metrics. A WAPE-improving model can still create plan movements that exceed the organization's ability to adapt. This paper proposes a forecast-to-plan feasibility gate for demand-planning deployment. The gate converts candidate forecasts into planning signals, measures planning-execution gap rate, and separates diagnostic forecast accuracy from operational planning utility. Across 123 paper-facing evaluation groups, the WAPE-best deployable strategy differs from the operational-loss-best deployable strategy in 91.1\% of cases. The accuracy-first planning-execution gap rate is 15.4\%, compared with 2.7\% for the best deployable operational-loss strategy. The results show that ensemble, smoothing, and DP-based policies occupy different deployment regimes: ensembles can dampen model-specific swings, smoothing slows adaptation when execution capacity is tight, and DP or budgeted DP supports governance when switching decisions have future consequences. The empirical contribution is not only that forecast accuracy is incomplete, but that the planning-execution gap can be measured and used as a deployment gate for forecast-driven planning strategies. WAPE should remain diagnostic; it should not be the sole deployment approval criterion.
